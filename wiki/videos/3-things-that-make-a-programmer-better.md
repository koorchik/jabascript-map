# 3 Things That Make a Programmer Better

> Original: "3 речі, які роблять програміста кращим" — https://youtu.be/wYkiKaT8whY

The channel's first video. Drawing on ~20 years of programming, a lot of interviewing, and launching many projects, the author skips the "banal" advice (soft skills, English) and names the three things he most often sees missing in otherwise strong coders: (1) a genuine desire to understand the **domain** ([[domain-knowledge]]) — the domain, not patterns, dictates the architecture; (2) learning how things work **deeply** ([[deep-learning-of-fundamentals]]) — he cites John Carmack as his authority on this; and (3) the ability to design and work with **[[abstractions]]**. Writing good code, he stresses, is only a fraction of being a good programmer.

## Key takeaways
- **Domain knowledge drives architecture.** His chat example: a designer shows you a chat with a typing indicator — do you reach for WebSockets? Not necessarily. If it's really just comments under orders, classic long-polling every ~5 seconds is fine; if it's an internal corporate chat with at most 100 users, 100 requests/second of polling may be simpler than a whole WebSocket setup. Whether that's acceptable you only know from the [[domain-knowledge|domain]].
- **Domain knowledge also drives motivation.** He was surprised to find programmers prefer domains they understand over technically challenging ones: friends wanted to work on an online store ("we get products, prices, discounts, orders") but lost motivation the moment the logistics module arrived — and regained it (plus made better architectural decisions) once they learned how logistics actually works as a business.
- To get better at domains he recommends the [[domain-driven-design|DDD books]]: Eric Evans and Vaughn Vernon (two books). Vernon is more practical, but he specifically recommends Evans — read it not for how to structure code (the examples/aggregates) but for *why*, for how the author thinks. Also: always think through the user's whole journey — you built the "Book ticket" button, but how does the user *get* the ticket? Is the event offline or online? What if the ticket is lost? These questions surface things the project manager never thought of, and it's cheaper to ask now.
- **Learn things deeply — there are two kinds of engineers on the market**: those who say "why should I know the internals, I just call the methods" and those willing to dig. If you don't know how [[mysql]], [[postgresql]] or MongoDB work inside, you don't know their *properties*: what happens if data doubles, what an `UPDATE` actually costs, how the engines differ ([[deep-learning-of-fundamentals]]).
- Classic shallow-knowledge traps he's seen personally: people who think [[jwt]] *encrypts* the payload (it's a signature — anyone can read it), and a client-side engineer who claimed HTTP Basic Auth was "encrypted because it's [[base64]]" — Base64 is [[encoding]], not [[encryption]]. Understanding [[asymmetric-encryption|asymmetric crypto]], keys and [[digital-signatures|signatures]] even at the "ABC" level leads to better decisions ([[security-practices]]).
- The "Docker is 2x slower on macOS" war story: a developer reported it as a bug; his answer — "that's not a bug, it's a feature." Containers don't actually exist as a kernel concept even in Linux — they're an abstraction over isolation subsystems. There's no [[docker|Docker]] on macOS at all: a Linux VM boots, your process runs inside it, and every filesystem call goes through the Linux kernel *and then* the host kernel — of course it costs performance and memory.
- Where to start digging: the classic interview question "what happens between typing a URL and the page rendering" can take you through the network stack, HTTP/HTTPS, OSI layers — and even [[dns]] hides depth (does Chrome itself query DNS, or delegate to another OS process?). Studying security (injections, XSS/CSRF, buffer overflow, Heartbleed, Shellshock) is a great vehicle because security is exactly about how things work and how to violate their expected properties. His concrete starter exercise (from webilab's internal 13–14-topic baseline): **write a JSON parser with zero libraries** — you'll understand JSON in detail.
- **Designing abstractions**: the two core aspects are coupling and cohesion (separate video promised). His personal method ([[software-design]]): he likes tests but not the TDD red-green loop; instead he never starts with the implementation — he writes *example usage code* for the future module/class first, and only when the API shows a single non-blurred responsibility, takes only the parameters it needs, doesn't leak implementation details, and looks extensible does he go implement it.
