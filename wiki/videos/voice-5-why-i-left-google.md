# Voice Message #5 — Why I Quit Google

> Original: "Голосове №5 - чому я звільнився з Google" — https://youtu.be/fEpoPbAbe48

A candid "voice message" episode — literally recorded in his car outside a hospital while waiting for his parents (his father had heart problems; his mother had cancer surgery three weeks earlier) — in which the author explains why, after ~4 years, he is leaving Google. The short version: he got everything he came for (confirmation that his level matches Google's Staff bar, the Google line in his CV, an inside view of how a huge corporation works) and he has catastrophically run out of time — for his cybersecurity PhD, volunteering, his parents and his own health; he counted the days per week and it simply didn't fit even part-time. Along the way he gives one of the most concrete public breakdowns of Google compensation mechanics (grants, refreshers and the 4-year cliff) and the story of how his most successful project — Application Canvas for Cloud Run — was noticed by top management, praised… and shut down, resetting his L6 promotion clock. See [[career-and-growth]], [[channel-and-author]].

## Key takeaways
- **What impressed him most at Google vs. Ukrainian outsourcing: total ownership.** From junior level up, the engineer who launches a feature writes the design doc, the code, the playbook/launch plan and the tests, organizes a 30–40 minute "bug bash" call for the team to click through the feature (there is no manual QA), launches behind feature flags, monitors the metrics and fixes the bugs — responsible end to end.
- **Google total compensation = salary + bonus (10–15%) + stock**, and the stock is a 4-year grant: the number of shares is fixed at the grant-date price (his example: $100k at $125/share; the stock later hit $200, then dropped to $167 when "Trump's policy" hit the markets) and vests monthly. Stock was roughly *half* his comp. Each year you get a refresher grant, but much smaller (his next-year refresh was ~3x smaller, the current one ~4x smaller than the starting grant) — so **after four years, without a promotion, everyone's comp drops back to roughly their entry-level package**. Meanwhile his UAH salary got +17% and +15% raises but declined steadily in dollar terms. That cliff, timed with the March bonus/salary review, was the rational moment to leave: "what Google now offers won't motivate me."
- **The promo war story:** he interviewed *for* Staff (L6); one interview went off the rails ("I just misunderstood the question and talked about the wrong thing"), so he joined as Senior with "you'll get your Staff quickly." A staff promotion requires a staff-level project, which normally takes 2–3 years. He spent two years building **Application Canvas** — drag Cloud Run services, databases and integrations onto a canvas, deploy the whole application, even merge two architectures — launched iteratively (first as "Cloud Run integrations"), featured in four Google Cloud Next presentations including keynotes, with thousands of paying customers. Then top management decided it was *so* good it should exist at the level of all of Google Cloud — closed the product, reused none of the technology, and his team spent a year deprecating and deleting everything he had ever built at Google. His promo packet was then rejected with "the product no longer exists — do it again," i.e. a ~3-year restart. A comparable new product might have earned L6 in ~1.5 years, but not part-time — so the promotion made staying pointless.
- **Promo-committee mechanics he found broken:** the people who evaluate you have never met you and must reconstruct your impact from design docs and testimonials; and since ~7 of the 8 evaluators were backend engineers judging frontend-heavy work, they struggled to see where the complexity was. His manager, director and neighboring teams called him "one of the most underrated employees."
- **Why he never switched to management** despite covering ~70% of the management work for his own team (they never even added a dedicated manager) and being repeatedly invited — promotion would have been *easier* on the management track: management at Google is mostly performance reviews and promo packets, a huge amount of hard work, and he explicitly went to Google *not* for management (he'd had plenty at WebbyLab). He's glad he refused.
- **What the 4 years taught him:** Google's internal RPC (gRPC-like) and heavy protobuf work; the internal build system (whose open-source version is Bazel); reading mountains of design docs — including how Google Cloud console's micro-frontends work (one of the largest Angular apps in the world, ~12 million lines of code); API design; writing design docs; frontend test harnesses (he gave a talk on this — "almost nobody does automated frontend testing properly"). He watched three reorgs in four years and was impressed that a company that size genuinely keeps changing priorities and re-pointing its best directors.
- **The skills transfer.** Google increasingly uses common technology — he wrote TypeScript, Angular, Java in a VS Code-based internal editor — so nothing locks the experience inside: testing approaches, design-doc habits and API design all carry over ([[vs-code]]).
- **Employment fine print:** he had dual Ukrainian/Polish contracts; the $13k signing bonus was payable only upon relocating to Poland, which the full-scale invasion made impossible — so he never received it. Under Ukrainian law he works two weeks' notice; offboarding looks simple, whereas onboarding was two months of no real work — lectures, a starter project, first commits to the monorepo.
- **He unreservedly recommends working at Google** — professional colleagues, a large trust credit (a team was formed around him for Cloud Run UI experience work, and he was being offered a sub-project to reshape the Cloud Run experience) — "but it depends on what you want to get out of it." His own exit plan: health first, then more YouTube, the PhD, volunteering, a flexible schedule in Kyiv and regular trips to his parents.
