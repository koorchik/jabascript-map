# Q&A and Plans for 2024

> Original: "Відповідаю на питання й розповім про плани на 2024" — https://youtu.be/gc9ha4wptuk

A 2+ hour livestream ("I thought it would be 30–40 minutes") with three threads: results of 2023, plans for 2024, and a marathon of audience questions. It is the most biographical video on the channel: the author (Viktor Turskyi) tells his full story — economist by education, in IT since 2003, infosec intern at BMS Consulting, dorm-network admin, Perl, Mail.ua, Portaone/VoIP, co-founding WebbyLab in 2011, and joining Google in 2021 as a Senior SWE on Google Cloud's Cloud Run team. He explains the channel's founding idea: an **evergreen Ukrainian-language knowledge base for engineers** — videos you can watch from the oldest onward without them going stale — and commits to keeping the content authentic and self-made (he even edits himself, and argues the OpenAI Sora announcement makes authentic experience-based content *more* valuable). Deep dives along the way: Google's ownership culture and level system, interview prep with AlgoExpert, total-comp and employment mechanics, the wartime Ukrainian IT market and why outstaffing is dying, how AI changes engineering jobs, his React/SSR war stories, composite database indexes, and his personal learning method. See [[channel-and-author]], [[career-and-growth]], [[ai-and-jobs]].

## Key takeaways

**Channel story and plans**
- This is his *second* YouTube attempt: he first ran a retro-gaming channel ("РетроГейм на релаксі") for about a year of weekly streams — a deliberate testing ground for OBS, lighting, mics and camera setups — and got only ~300 subscribers. The programming channel reached ~19,000 in one year; 167 sponsors at stream time. Sponsor content is "deeper dives", but anything important for everyone stays public (the DHCP video started as sponsor-only; he opened it up and it hit 70k views).
- Plans for 2024: a proper HTTPS explainer (he hasn't seen even an English video that is simple, useful *and* deep), a Google Cloud / Cloud Run series, crossing 20–25k subscribers, and first sponsorship integrations — but only for products he already recommends anyway (the first is Mate Academy, whose bootcamp-style courses with a pay-a-percent-of-salary-after-hiring model he has long recommended; casino/blockchain offers get an instant "no").
- He refuses to outsource editing or hire a team: authenticity is the moat, and editing his own videos taught him to optimize *recording* (he now draws/writes live in OBS instead of splicing tablet footage in post).

**Biography and Google** ([[channel-and-author]], [[career-and-growth]])
- Path: economics degree (Shevchenko University) → infosec internship at BMS Consulting in 2003 (turned down a 5-year contract out of fear he'd stay an economist) → built and admin'd a 250-computer dorm network with Perl scripts talking to switches → Mail.ua for 4 years (custom FUSE-based filesystem, PXE-based zero-touch server deployment "before all this existed") → Portaone (VoIP: SIP, H.323, RTMP, RADIUS, billing) → hit a growth ceiling and co-founded WebbyLab in 2011 with Anton Morozov → Google offer process took ~half a year (mostly waiting), joined 2021.
- Google hires no remote positions; his Ukraine presence is an accident of history — a COVID-era "split start" dual contract (Ukraine + Poland) with a relocation deadline that COVID, then the full-scale invasion, kept deferring. Employment is strictly по трудовій (payroll) in both Ukraine and Poland — no ФОП — so net pay can be lower than a contractor's.
- Interviewed at L6 (Staff): some interviews got "best I've ever conducted" feedback, one went sideways, so he was hired as Senior with "join and you'll quickly get L6" — 2.5 years later he still doesn't have it, and he knows L4s who left Google for Senior titles elsewhere who were unquestionably seniors. The system isn't perfect. He recommends [[the-staff-engineers-path]] for what the staff role actually is.
- **Level ≠ technology knowledge.** At Google knowing a stack means nothing for your level — he had never written Java or Angular and barely TypeScript before joining, and that's what he now works in. Level is determined by **design skill**, which rests on working with [[abstractions]] and understanding how things work inside to know their *properties* (his example: you needn't read index implementation code, but you must know an index is a sorted structure — hence writes get expensive, hence what queries it can serve) — see [[software-design]], [[deep-learning-of-fundamentals]].
- Google culture: end-to-end **ownership** — even a junior gets a mini-project as one sentence and owns design doc, sign-offs with PM/designers, delivery, production launch, experiment and dashboards. There are no project managers; engineering managers exist to make the team effective and grow people, but going to negotiate with another team is *your* job. What shocked him: the infrastructure scale — his personal dev VM has tens of cores and 192 GB RAM, needed just to run the frontend (~50–60 GB), because Google Cloud console is one of the world's largest web applications. His actual weekdays: ~13 hours of meetings per week; standard time to onboard to full performance is ~6 months.
- Interview prep: 6 interviews + 3 team-fit calls. He prepped the coding part for two weeks, every morning 1–2 tasks on **AlgoExpert** (~$100, made by an ex-Googler; "the best resource I've seen", no sponsorship) — solved ~50–70 of the then-100 problems. None of them appeared in the real interviews, but the practice built the thinking habit. Key advice: narrate your thought process, learn to test code in your head. He passed with a *non-optimal* solution — but he found two bugs himself, fixed them, named the complexity and pointed out the shortcomings.
- Compensation reasoning: he had offers with substantially higher pay than Google and treats jobs as **investments in himself** — "Google says it targets ~80th percentile of market, so one in five offers will beat it; if you can pass Google, you can pass four more interviews." Gray areas like blockchain can pay 1.5x. Money as goal is like breathing: necessary, not the purpose.
- Post-35 coding: he turns 40 on March 15 and coding isn't harder — the brain adapts fine (he re-checked: still reads a Linux architecture book in two days on a train); what changes is that seniority piles on non-coding duties.

**Wartime IT market and business**
- The market is bad: engineer salaries down ~20%, hundreds of CVs per junior opening — easy to hire, hard to get hired. The real bottleneck is *sales*: clients fear mobilization mid-project, and founders can't travel to meet customers while foreign competitors can. WebbyLab (~115 people) is shrinking, not growing; first year of the war they donated **100% of profit (~10M UAH) to the army**, then switched to a matching **volunteer fund fed by 50% of profit**.
- Why outstaffing in Ukraine is dying: mass layoffs flooded the market with engineers; foreign clients learned to hire Ukrainians directly via ФОП; hiring platforms disintermediate; mobilization risk attaches to a specific person; and remote work killed the office's value.
- Starting an IT company now is "insane risk". His own experience: the first five years of WebbyLab he and his co-founder earned *less* than salaried jobs, working 10–12 hours with no weekends — 10 engineers at $3k salaries means selling $50k of projects every month or dying. Business demands total focus; his one attempt at moonlight freelancing netted $100 for a whole summer of evenings. For the decision itself he cites **Bezos's regret-minimization framework**.
- Getting reserved from mobilization (бронювання): even at Google only a small fraction of specialists get it — your military specialty must qualify, documents must be in order, and the company needs "strategically important" status; the process can fail at any step.

**AI** ([[ai-coding-agents]], [[ai-and-jobs]])
- He uses ChatGPT (or Google Bard — he tested Bard Advanced and finds it no worse than GPT-4; "renamed Gemini" mid-stream) constantly: brainstorming, generating five candidate variable names, parsing a binary format, explaining pasted code or Unix command output, and replacing man pages — his ffmpeg example: it walked him through the generate-palette-then-encode trick that shrank a GIF ~100x. Another: it showed him how to attach a WebSocket server to his existing Express HTTP server via the upgrade mechanism instead of opening a new port. Google's internal Copilot analog is trained on Google's codebase and autocompletes in-style; the real GitHub Copilot he subscribed to, tried, and unsubscribed.
- His position: every engineer must start using AI tools now or lose competitiveness — "the first thing a beginner should install is a code editor; actually maybe first register a ChatGPT/Bard account."
- Will AI kill programming jobs? His Google Translate analogy: translators' demand fell and productivity rose; same for designers and copywriters. But **code's only consumer is programmers** — clients want a working product, not code — so LLMs don't replace engineers, they amplify them. An engineer's job starts at understanding a business-domain problem and proposing a technical solution, through delivery. Juniors won't disappear either: you have to grow mids and seniors from somewhere; juniors just must be more productive using these tools.
- ML engineering as a career is permanent research-mode: even Google's best ML engineers don't know whether something will work until a proof of concept exists, and many engineers find that uncertainty miserable. Building an AI *technology* startup now is "a road to nowhere" — big players outrun you; the engineering opportunity is composing existing models/APIs (yesterday text-to-speech was impossible, today it's an API call) into working systems.

**Technology takes**
- React war story: WebbyLab had React 0.4 in production two months after its release, when interviewees hadn't heard of it. He won *react.js Newsletter*'s worldwide best-article contest with a piece on **server-side rendering** — not a hello-world but real production problems (based on his KharkivJS talk) — which won him entry to ReactConf (tickets were lottery-only, ~400–500 seats) and an afterparty with the React team including vjeux. SSR remains "a great idea", especially for SEO.
- **Next.js**: he loves it (mytalks.com is built on it) — isomorphic apps, SEO, fast loads — but criticizes the internal architecture: he couldn't parametrize the static-page cache to put it on S3.
- **Cloud Run**: he moved all his pet projects from DigitalOcean to [[google-cloud-run]] because it's much cheaper (a small service costs a couple of dollars). Cloud Functions v2 *is* a Cloud Run container under the hood — Google abandoned the separate implementation. To estimate cost: don't trust the request-count calculator; simulate a day of traffic and multiply by 30.
- Composite [[database-indexes]] explained on the fly: an index is "copy the column and sort it"; a two-column index is a two-level sort, so it serves lookups on (A) and (A,B) but not (B) alone; it can also act as a covering index (select served entirely from the index without hitting the table); selectivity determines how effective it is. Two separate indexes = two independently sorted copies, searchable in any order.
- Language picks: for beginners, one language — JavaScript+TypeScript or Python. Go is fine for microservices, proxies, CLI tools and system things, but "absolutely not okay" for e.g. an accounting system — it lacks the abstractions to express business logic the way he wants. Kotlin > Java ("Java is pain"); C# he likes more than Java; PHP is a legitimate business/e-commerce language but losing popularity; Bun vs Node — Node.js wins for learning because it's the standard environment with more docs, more Stack Overflow, and ChatGPT knows it better.
- OS/gadgets: he streams from Kubuntu and loves Linux most because containers are a Linux-kernel feature (namespaces, cgroups) — real containers only exist in Linux; elsewhere Docker means a VM. Two phones (prefers Android; iPhone bought for the in-car experience and video quality), Windows for games, a Google MacBook for work. All channel videos were shot on an iPhone 13 Pro Max via the NDI Camera app; he recently switched to a Fujifilm X-E4 with a used 15-45 lens bought for 5,000 UAH — better picture than the phone, while a Logitech StreamCam was far worse than either.
- Information [[security-practices]] framing: "almost all of infosec is understanding how things work inside" — that's why the channel explains protocols; the DHCP-in-a-café video was deliberately framed as a security story. When you know internals you know properties — and how to break them.

**How he learns** ([[deep-learning-of-fundamentals]])
- "Useful procrastination": when he needs a new domain (his example: camera color science — N-log/S-log, subsampling, sensors, lenses), he binge-watches many videos from different angles until saturation, then switches to practice. Reading the 10th article on a topic yields 5% new information but a new angle each time.
- The knowledge-tree principle (from his earlier video): we only remember what hooks onto existing knowledge, so he deliberately connects anything new to the maximum of what he already knows — like a web catching it.
- Learning a new technology or switching stacks = theory (a book or good course) + a pet project **you actually want to launch**, ideally with a "virtual customer" — a friend or colleague who wants the result — and no deadline commitments. That beats any certificate at interview time.
- Books beat articles for depth: an article matching a *middle* chapter of a book can't find an audience because readers lack the context the earlier chapters build. For web engineers he singles out [[high-performance-browser-networking]] — free online, by a Google engineer, "explains the speed-of-light stuff and much more."
- Recommended fellow channel: Serhii Babych's "Дивовижний світ веброзробки" (interview/CSS content — "expect no CSS videos from me, ever"); his own deep technical talks to look up: the Excel-on-JavaScript architecture talks (OdessaJS, DOU "1-2-3 Tech" interview) and his frontend test-harness talk at Framework Days.
