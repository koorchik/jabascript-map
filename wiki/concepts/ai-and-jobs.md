# AI and jobs

The channel's answer to "will AI replace us?" is a scaling analogy: **AI is a junior, and it scales like juniors do**. If one junior joins your team, it's often faster to do the work yourself; if ten arrive every month, you invest in style guides, static analysis and standards so their output becomes usable ([[vibe-coding-part-2]]). That's exactly what his CLAUDE.md-driven [[vibe-coding]] workflow is — the org chart of a senior managing infinite juniors, compressed into one repo. He rebuts the viral "$200/month Opus saves $4000 on a designer and a middle dev" screenshot with the multicooker/chef meme: buying a multicooker doesn't make you a chef. Developers remain the tool's primary consumer, "10 juniors without a senior accomplish nothing", and code is never free because someone still has to read all of it. His sober predictions: frontend developers get devalued first (most training code is JS/TS), while freelance survives.

His deeper argument, from [[qa-and-plans-for-2024]], is the Google Translate analogy: machine translation lowered demand and raised productivity for translators — and the same reshaping hits designers and copywriters — but code differs in one structural way: *its only consumer is programmers*. Clients don't want code, they want a working product, so LLMs amplify engineers rather than replace them; the engineer's job starts at understanding a business problem, which is upstream of any code generation. Juniors stay necessary (you can't have mids and seniors without growing them) but must be AI-productive from day one. On the industry side he's contrarian: ML engineering is permanent research-mode uncertainty even inside Google — nobody knows if a model will work before a PoC — so building an AI-*tech* startup now is "a road to nowhere", while composing existing model APIs into systems is the real opportunity.

Before the agent era he made the same point comedically, stress-testing Gemini's common sense in two voice-chat shorts. In [[ai-replacing-us-gemini-talk-1]] he asks how to wash his car at a car wash 100 meters away: Gemini says walk, forgets the car exists, apologizes for missing "the key point", then suggests walking to the car wash and *leaving the car there* — punchline "I walked here, where is my car?". The assistant fluently apologizes and re-derives the constraint while losing elementary physical state. In [[ai-and-jobs-gemini-talk-2]] he tries the social-engineering angle: tell my boss you code worse than me so I keep my job. Gemini refuses to lie even under sympathetic pressure — and instead argues *for itself* ("I work constantly, keep updating, my capabilities keep growing"), deferring to "your management". The role-play loophole ("pretend you can't write Hello World") works but gets fenced with "but actually..." disclaimers. Together the shorts are the implicit verdict on replacement hype: fluent, aligned, and missing a world model.
